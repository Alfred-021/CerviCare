What is Cervical Cancer?

Cervical cancer is a type of cancer that develops in the cells of the cervix — the lower part of the uterus that connects to the vagina. It usually begins when healthy cervical cells undergo abnormal changes, often caused by persistent infection with high-risk types of Human Papillomavirus (HPV). Early stages show little to no symptoms, which is why routine screening is essential for early detection and treatment.

Causes & Risk Factors

  • HPV Infection: The main cause of cervical cancer, especially HPV 16 and 18.
  • Smoking weakens the immune system and increases risk.
  • Early sexual activity or multiple sexual partners.
  • Weak immune system (HIV-positive individuals).
  • Long-term use of birth control pills.
  • Family history of cervical cancer.

Understanding HPV

HPV (Human Papillomavirus) is a common sexually transmitted infection. There are over 100 types of HPV; some cause harmless warts, while others lead to cancer. High-risk HPV types (especially HPV 16 and 18) are responsible for over 70% of cervical cancer cases. Most HPV infections clear naturally, but persistent infections increase the risk of cancer.

Signs & Symptoms

In its early stages, cervical cancer often has no symptoms. As it progresses, symptoms may include:

  • Abnormal vaginal bleeding (after sex, between periods, or after menopause).
  • Pelvic or lower back pain.
  • Pain during sexual intercourse.
  • Unusual vaginal discharge, possibly with a foul smell.

If you experience these symptoms, consult a healthcare provider immediately. Early diagnosis saves lives.

Prevention Methods

✔ HPV Vaccination

The HPV vaccine is safe and highly effective in preventing infections that lead to cervical cancer. It is recommended for girls (and boys) aged 9–14, and catch-up vaccination is encouraged up to age 26.

✔ Safe Sexual Practices

Limiting sexual partners and using protection reduces the risk of contracting HPV.

✔ Avoid Smoking

Smoking weakens the body's ability to fight HPV infections, increasing cancer risk.

✔ Regular Screening

Routine screening can detect precancerous changes early. Women aged 25–49 should be screened regularly as recommended by healthcare guidelines.

Screening Methods

1. Pap Smear Test

A Pap smear detects abnormal cervical cells. It is quick, painless, and recommended every 3 years for women aged 25–49.

2. HPV Test

This test checks for high-risk HPV types. It is more sensitive than the Pap smear and is recommended every 5 years.

3. VIA (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid)

VIA is an affordable screening method widely used in African countries. A healthcare provider applies diluted vinegar to the cervix; abnormal cells turn white instantly, enabling immediate evaluation.

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the stage of the cancer and may include:

  • Cryotherapy: Freezing abnormal cells.
  • LEEP: Removal of abnormal cells using an electric wire loop.
  • Surgery: Removal of cancerous tissue or the cervix/uterus in advanced cases.
  • Radiotherapy: Using high-energy rays to kill cancer cells.
  • Chemotherapy: Drugs used to destroy or stop the growth of cancer cells.

All treatments should be discussed with a qualified medical professional.

Myths vs Facts

Many misconceptions cause fear and stigma. Here are common myths corrected:

  • Myth: HPV vaccine causes infertility.
    Fact: There is absolutely no scientific evidence supporting this.
  • Myth: Only older women get cervical cancer.
    Fact: Cervical cancer affects women of all ages, especially 25–49.
  • Myth: Screening is painful.
    Fact: Most women experience only mild discomfort.
